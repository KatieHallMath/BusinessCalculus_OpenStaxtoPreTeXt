Proof

Let \(P={x_i},i=0,1,\dots, n\) be a regular partition of \([a,b].\) Then, we can write

\begin{align*} F(b)-F(a)\amp =F(x_n)-F(x_0)\\ \amp =[F(x_n)-F(x_{n-1})]+[F(x_{n-1})-F(x_{n-2})]+\dots +[F(x_1)-F(x_0)]\\ \amp =\displaystyle{ \sum_{i=1}^n }[F(x_i)-F(x_{i-1})]. \end{align*}

Now, we know \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) over \([a,b],\) so by the Mean Value Theorem (see The Mean Value TheoremÂ 4.3) for \(i=0,1,\dots, n\) we can find \(c_i\) in \([x_{i-1},x_i]\) such that

\begin{equation*} F(x_i)-F(x_{i-1})=F'(c_i)(x_i-x_{i-1})=f(c_i)\Delta x. \end{equation*}

Then, substituting into the previous equation, we have

\begin{equation*} F(b)-F(a)=\displaystyle{ \sum_{i=1}^nf(c_i)\Delta x}. \end{equation*}

Taking the limit of both sides as \(n\to \infty ,\) we obtain

\begin{align*} F(b)-F(a)\amp =\lim_{n\to \infty }\displaystyle{ \sum_{i=1}^nf(c_i)\Delta x}\\ \amp =\displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx}. \end{align*}
