Solution 4.4.1.

We know that

\begin{equation} E(p)= \dfrac{-p}{x}\dfrac{dx}{dp} .\tag{4.1.2} \end{equation}

To find \(\frac{dx}{dp},\) we need to rewrite the demand equation so that it is of the form \(x=f(p)\text{.}\) We get

\begin{align*} p \amp = 2-x \\ x+p \amp = 2 \\ x \amp = 2-p \end{align*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dp}=\frac{d}{dp}(2-p)=-1. \end{equation*}

Plugging in to (4.1.2), we get that

\begin{equation*} E(p)= \dfrac{-p}{x}\dfrac{dx}{dp}=-\dfrac{p}{2-p}\cdot (-1) =\frac{p}{2-p}. \end{equation*}

Now we can evaluate \(E(p)\) at each of the given \(p \) values.

\begin{align*} E(0.75) \amp =\frac{0.75}{2-0.75}=0.6 \amp \amp E\lt 1, \text{ inelastic } \\ E(1.50) \amp =\frac{1.50}{2-1.50}=3 \amp \amp E>1, \text{ elastic }\\ E(1.00) \amp =\frac{1.00}{2-1.00}=1\amp \amp E=1, \text{ unit elastic } \end{align*}
