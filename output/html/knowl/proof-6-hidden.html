Since \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([a,b],\) by the extreme value theorem (see Maxima and Minima 4.2), it assumes minimum and maximum values—\(m\) and \(M\text{,}\) respectively—on \([a,b].\) Then, for all \(x\) in \([a,b],\) we have \(m\leq f(x)\leq M.\) Therefore, by the comparison theorem (see The Definite Integral 5.2 ), we have

\begin{equation*} m(b-a)\leq \displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx}\leq M(b-a). \end{equation*}

Dividing by \(b-a\) gives us

\begin{equation*} m\leq \frac{1}{b-a} \displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx}\leq M. \end{equation*}

Since \(\frac{1}{b-a} \displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{b} f}(x)dx\) is a number between \(m\) and \(M\text{,}\) and since \(f(x)\) is continuous and assumes the values \(m\) and \(M\) over \([a,b],\) by the Intermediate Value Theorem (see Continuity 2.4), there is a number \(c\) over \([a,b]\) such that

\begin{equation*} f(c)=\frac{1}{b-a} \displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx}, \end{equation*}

and the proof is complete.
