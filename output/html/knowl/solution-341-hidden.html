If we let \(u=x-1,\) then \(du=dx.\) But this does not account for the \(x\) in the numerator of the integrand. We need to express \(x\) in terms of \(u\text{.}\) If \(u=x-1,\) then \(x=u+1.\) Now we can rewrite the integral in terms of \(u\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \displaystyle{ \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{x-1}}dx}\amp =\displaystyle{ \int \frac{u+1}{\sqrt{u}}du}\\ \amp =\displaystyle{ \int \sqrt{u}+\frac{1}{\sqrt{u}}du}\\ \amp =\displaystyle{ \int (u^{1/2}+u^{-1/2})du}. \end{align*}

Then we integrate in the usual way, replace \(u\) with the original expression, and factor and simplify the result. Thus,

\begin{align*} \displaystyle{ \int (u^{1/2}+u^{-1/2})du}\amp =\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}+2u^{1/2}+C\\ \amp =\frac{2}{3}(x-1)^{3/2}+2(x-1)^{1/2}+C\\ \amp =(x-1)^{1/2}\left[\frac{2}{3}(x-1)+2\right]+C\\ \amp =(x-1)^{1/2}\left(\frac{2}{3}x-\frac{2}{3}+\frac{6}{3}\right)\\ \amp =(x-1)^{1/2}\left(\frac{2}{3}x+\frac{4}{3}\right)\\ \amp =\frac{2}{3}(x-1)^{1/2}(x+2)+C. \end{align*}
