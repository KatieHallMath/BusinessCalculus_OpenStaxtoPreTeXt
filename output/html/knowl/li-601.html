Item 3.
If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator \((n\gt m),\) then \(f\) does not have a horizontal asymptote. The limits at infinity are either positive or negative infinity, depending on the signs of the leading terms. In addition, using long division, the function can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=g(x)+\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}, \end{equation*}
where the degree of \(r(x)\) is less than the degree of \(q(x).\) As a result, \(\displaystyle{\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}r(x)/q(x)=0.}\) Therefore, the values of \([f(x)-g(x)]\) approach zero as \(x\to\pm\infty.\) Now let's consider the end behavior for functions involving a radical.
