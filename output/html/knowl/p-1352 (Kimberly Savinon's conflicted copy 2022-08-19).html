We conclude that we can determine the concavity of a function \(f\) by looking at the second derivative of \(f.\) In addition, we observe that a function \(f\) can switch concavity (FigureÂ 4.73). However, a continuous function can switch concavity only at a point \(x\) if \(f''(x)=0\) or \(f''(x)\) is undefined. Consequently, to determine the intervals where a function \(f\) is concave up and concave down, we look for those values of \(x\) where \(f''(x)=0\) or \(f''(x)\) is undefined. When we have determined these points, we divide the domain of \(f\) into smaller intervals and determine the sign of \(f''\) over each of these smaller intervals. If \(f''\) changes sign as we pass through a point \(x,\) then \(f\) changes concavity. It is important to remember that a function \(f\) may not change concavity at a point \(x\) even if \(f''(x)=0\) or \(f''(x)\) is undefined. If, however, \(f\) does change concavity at a point \(a\) and \(f\) is continuous at \(a,\) we say the point \((a,f(a))\) is an inflection point of \(f.\)
