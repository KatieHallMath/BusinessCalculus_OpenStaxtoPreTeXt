Applying the definition of the derivative, we have

\begin{align*} F'(x)\amp =\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{h}\left[\displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{x+h} f(t)dt}-\displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{x} f(t)dt}\right]\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{h}\left[\displaystyle{ \int_{a}^{x+h} f(t)dt}+\displaystyle{ \int_{x}^{a} f(t)dt}\right]\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{h}\displaystyle{ \int_{x}^{x+h} f(t)dt}. \end{align*}

Looking carefully at this last expression, we see \(\frac{1}{h}\displaystyle{ \int_{x}^{x+h} f(t)dt}\) is just the average value of the function \(f(x)\) over the interval \([x,x+h].\) Therefore, by TheoremÂ 5.68, there is some number \(c\) in \([x,x+h]\) such that

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{h}\displaystyle{ \int_{x}^{x+h} f(x)dx}=f(c). \end{equation*}

In addition, since \(c\) is between \(x\) and \(x\) + \(h\text{,}\) \(c\) approaches \(x\) as \(h\) approaches zero. Also, since \(f(x)\) is continuous, we have \(\lim_{h\to 0}f(c)=\lim_{c\to x}f(c)=f(x).\) Putting all these pieces together, we have

\begin{align*} F'(x) \amp =\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1}{h}\displaystyle{ \int_{x}^{x+h} f(x)dx}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}f(c)\\ \amp =f(x), \end{align*}

and the proof is complete.
