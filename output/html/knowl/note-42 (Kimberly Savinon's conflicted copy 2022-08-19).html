Note 5.42. Rule: Properties of the Definite Integral.

  1. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{a}^{a} f(x)dx=0} \end{equation*}
    If the limits of integration are the same, the integral is just a line and contains no area.

  2. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{b}^{a} f(x)dx} =-\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx} \end{equation*}
    If the limits are reversed, then place a negative sign in front of the integral.

  3. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} [f(x)+g(x)]dx} =\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx} +\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} g(x)dx} \end{equation*}
    The integral of a sum is the sum of the integrals.

  4. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} [f(x)-g(x)]dx=\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx-\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} g(x)dx} } } \end{equation*}
    The integral of a difference is the difference of the integrals.

  5. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} cf(x)dx} =c\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} f(x)} \end{equation*}
    for constant \(c\text{.}\) The integral of the product of a constant and a function is equal to the constant multiplied by the integral of the function.

  6. \begin{equation*} \displaystyle{\int_{a}^{b} f(x)dx} =\displaystyle{\int_{a}^{c} f(x)dx} +\displaystyle{\int_{c}^{b} f(x)dx} \end{equation*}
    Although this formula normally applies when \(c\) is between \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) the formula holds for all values of \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\text{,}\) provided \(f(x)\) is integrable on the largest interval.
