Let’s begin by trying to calculate \(f(3).\)

\begin{equation*} f(3)=-(3^2)+4=-5. \end{equation*}

Thus, \(f(3)\) is defined. Next, we calculate \(\displaystyle{\lim_{x\to 3}f(x).}\) To do this, we must compute \(\displaystyle{\lim_{x\to 3^-}f(x)}\) and \(\displaystyle{\lim_{x\to 3^+}f(x)}\text{ : }\)

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3^-}f(x)=-(3^2)+4=-5 \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3^+}f(x)=4(3)-8=4. \end{equation*}

Therefore, \(\displaystyle{\lim_{x\to 3}f(x)}\) does not exist. Thus, \(f(x)\) is not continuous at 3. The graph of \(f(x)\) is shown in Figure 2.97.
